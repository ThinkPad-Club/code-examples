# Daily Coding Demonstrations 🚀

Each ThinkPad Club meeting follows a similar structure so everyone knows what to expect.

The goal is simple: **learn something new, try your best, and build cool things.**

---

# 1. Live Coding Demonstration

We begin each meeting with a **live coding demonstration**.

During the demonstration, the instructor will build something step-by-step while explaining what is happening. This might be:

- a game  
- an animation  
- a song  
- an interesting algorithm  
- or another creative coding project  

Students are encouraged to **follow along on their own computer**.

You do **not** need to keep up perfectly.

If something goes wrong or you get lost, **that is completely normal and 100% okay**.

All demonstration code will be available in the **ThinkPad Club resources repository**, and students may open the example code to use as a reference while they work.

The most important thing is to **try**.

You do **not** have to finish the project.

---

# 2. Questions and Debugging Help

After the demonstration:

- The demonstration may repeat
- Students may ask questions
- The instructor may help students **debug their programs**
- Students may continue experimenting with the project

Programming involves making mistakes and fixing them.  
That process is part of how programmers learn.

---

# 3. Submitting Your Project

At the end of the session, students will be asked to **submit their project**.

Projects may be **finished or unfinished**.

Submissions may go to:

- the **class GitHub**
- the **class website**

depending on the type of project.

The goal is to **practice sharing work the way real programmers do**.

---

# Topics We May Explore

Demonstrations may include topics such as:

- Coding websites with **HTML / CSS**
- Coding foundations with **JavaScript**
- Data structures and algorithms with **Python**
- Game development and animation with **Scratch**
- JavaScript art with **p5.js**
- Coding music with **Strudel**
- Building AI and data tools with **Google Colab**
- Game development for the **Thumby**
- Python basics and drawing with **Trinket**
- AI app development with **Hugging Face / Gradio**
- Professional coding literacy with **GitHub**
- AI literacy demonstrations using tools like **Claude**

---

# 4. Elective Projects (After Submitting)

Students who **successfully complete and submit the demonstration project** for the day (after instructor review and approval) may choose from **elective activities**.

These are organized into **kits or stations** and usually allow only:

- one student  
- or very small groups  

depending on materials and instructor approval.

Elective activities may include:

- Game Boy development with **GB Studio**
- Game development with **Lua / Playdate**
- Building a personal website on a **Linux / Raspberry Pi server**
- Python robotics with **LEGO Scratch**
- Studying electronics by **taking apart old devices**
- Building circuits on **breadboards with electronic components**
- Exploring **analog electronic music** with instruments like the Arturia MicroBrute

Electives are a chance to **explore deeper projects and specialized tools**.

Availability may depend on **materials and classroom conduct**.

---

# The Most Important Rule

Try your best.  
Be patient with yourself.  
Help others when you can.

Every programmer — even professionals — spends a lot of time **experimenting, debugging, and learning step by step**.

That's exactly what ThinkPad Club is for. 💻🌻
